White Space and Clean Design

Quicksilver

quicksilver.com

Principle Definition

Whitespace, many times referred to as negative space, is the portion of a page left unmarked, the portion that is left blank, or (as Mark would quote) the empty space in a page. In web design terms, it’s the space between graphics, columns, images, text, margins and other elements. It is the space left untouched in order to smooth things out and transform a page into something elegant. It is also the blank space that reminds us that simpler designs are beautiful and that we don’t need to create a layout filled with text and graphical elements to deliver a clear and direct message.

Principle Application

White space is an important tool to use in website development. It is often difficult to digest all of the content on a page as a user and whitespace makes it easy to do so quickly. The portion of the page without content is just as important as those portions with content. Whitespace does a great job of emphasizing the content around it. Quicksilver does an awesome job utilizing the whitespace to emphasize 3 main ideas. It's clear because of whitespace that Quicksilver cares to establish their brand as a surf shop/appear company. Also it's clear that Quicksilver cares to let us know they have a ton of sales going on and an easy way to look into the sales. finally the clear design and whitespace is also emphasizing its search features so that users can quickly and easily look into the products

Visual Hierarchy

Protest

http://www.protest.eu

Principle Definition

Visual hierarchy is the arrangement of graphic elements in a design in order of importance of each element. The visual weight defines the importance of an element in a design’s hierarchy, communicating to a viewer’s eyes what to focus on and in what order.

Principle Application

Protest is a sportswear company with a wide range of apparel. Protest uses visual hierarchy on the website to captivate users to peak interest in the products. Protest also uses visual hierarchy to direct users to important features that will help them navigate and discover more of the site. This is done by following eye movement patterns. Our eyes naturally follow certain movement patterns and when designing by those patterns the users automatically are drawn to specific elements. The most common pattern is the F pattern because this is how we read a book. Another pattern is the Z pattern which is more often used with designs that rely on images more because the brain processes images faster than text. Protest uses a Z pattern for the website. The users are drawn to the main picture in the center of the site which is meant to peak interest before navigating through the site. Following the Z pattern Protest then prioritizes searching interfaces and functions. This is to make finding a product easy for the user.

PARC: Repetition

Feed

http://feedprojects.com

Principle Definition

Repetition gives the image order from repeated use of the same fonts, colors, layouts. This helps to orientate the viewer and also subtly show how certain ideas may be emphasized through reappearances of the same or a similar object.

Principle Application

Feed is a designer tote company that gives back by feeding the hungry for every bag bought. Feed makes very good use of repetition which is the "R" in the PARC principles. Essentially only totes are sold but totes vary widely in design and price. Repetition is able to simplify the implementation of the site along with providing a comfortable and easy to get familiar with user interface.
